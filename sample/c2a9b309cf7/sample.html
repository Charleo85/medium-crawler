My Real Life Superpower

This is not a metaphor. Take what follows literally.

When I was a kid, as far back as I can remember (I learned to read when I was 6 years old, so really, starting then), reading fiction was a fully immersive 3-D experience.

immersive
Adjective
generating a three-dimensional image which appears to surround the user — Dictionary.com

I’d get one or two paragraphs into a book, and all sense of myself physically sitting in a chair or sprawled on the bed, holding a book, reading words, my fingers turning pages, would simply vanish. Of course, I was still doing those things — sitting or lying down, reading black words on white pages, turning those pages — but in my mind, in my experience of myself, all of that disappeared. In its place, the story I was reading spun up fully-formed into a three dimensional movie taking place before me with all the reality of a super-vivid dream. It wasn’t so much that I experienced the outside world transforming into the story, like my brain was somehow projecting the scenes and characters out. Rather, what I read became a 3-Dimensional “mind-movie” that somehow replaced my experience of “me.” Internally, I became what I read. The outside world couldn’t compete, and simply faded into nothing.

Should someone call my name or tap me on the shoulder, I would immediately snap back to normal awareness and respond appropriately. So, if I was reading in the throes of some kind of kid self-hypnosis, it was a light trance that was easily broken — but it was also one that was easily reestablished. Once the distraction was dealt with, I could reliably, every time, return to my book and within a paragraph or two be right back in that state of total cinematic immersion.

I took it for granted that this was how everyone experienced reading. I thought kids in school who threw fits about library day and said they hated books were crazy. That was like hating movies or TV. What kid hates TV?

I joke as an adult that I learned more from reading science fiction (and fantasy) growing up than I did from twelve years of public education — but that’s actually a true statement, and my superpower is why. By the age of 16, I had not simply read, but lived every SciFi and fantasy novel in my small town public library (hundreds, at least). I joined the Science Fiction Book Club to score new “mind-journeys.” The walls of my room throughout my teen years were stacked floor to ceiling with used SciFi paperbacks. I don’t know how other kids spent their evenings, weekends and summer vacations, but I was traversing the stars, discovering alien civilizations, witnessing magic in action.

The last time I remember reading this way I was 19 years old, hungrily devouring the spiritual memoirs of Carlos Castaneda — books which read like great fiction, and which a lot of people say actually are fiction, though that remains an open question.

I was working as a janitor in a shopping mall pet store, a job that included the daily cleaning of a whole wall of dog kennels. It was a nasty job that had to be done in the wee hours of the morning, before the store or the mall opened for business. Nobody wants to buy a dog that poops, so we did our best to keep all evidence of bodily function safely hidden from public view (or smell). I’d arrive at 4:00 AM and have all the kennels scrubbed and disinfected, and the dogs fed, brushed and happy by 7:00. Then I’d have an hour to myself before the daytime sales crew arrived in their clean smocks and smiles to open the store. I spent that hour, every morning, for all appearances sitting cross-legged on the stockroom floor, reading — but I was really in the Sonoran Desert, learning Toltec sorcery alongside Carlos, from his Yaqui teacher don Juan Matus. A two paragraph descent into any of Castaneda’s books was all it took to make the whole mall dissolve to nothing around me. I remember the experience vividly — my final memory of reading any book that way.

I don’t know exactly when or why my youthful reading superpower ended. By the age of 25, as a late-blooming college Freshman working toward an English Literature degree, I distinctly remember reading lots of black words on white pages, arranged into sentences and paragraphs, aware of every page turn. Somewhere in my early 20s, my experience of reading was transformed from a cinematic spectacle that eclipsed the world into simply reading:

Reading requires one to identify and understand strings of words in a fluid manner. It is a detailed process that includes comprehension, word recognition, engagement, and fluency. — Study.com

My wife, who is a prolific reader of both fiction and non-fiction, and who devours easily five books for every one that I read, tells me she has never experienced 3-D immersive reading the way I remember it, not even as a kid. She’s never heard anyone else describe their experience of reading this way, either.

And now that I think about it, neither have I. I feel like I was born with a superpower I didn’t realize at the time was special, and that faded before I could comprehend or fully appreciate it.

I want my superpower back! I’m setting a goal this year to research “reading and the brain,” and if possible, to regain the ability to experience fiction with full 3-D cinematic immersion. If I figure it out, I promise I’ll share the secret here on Medium. We can all be superheroes and traverse the stars together.

So help me out, Medium friends. How do you experience reading? Has that experience changed over the years? Have you experienced 3-D immersive reading as described in this essay? Did it occur as a child or as an adult (or both)? Are you aware of books or programs that explore, and maybe even teach this kind of immersive reading?

I look forward to your responses!
